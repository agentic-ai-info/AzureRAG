Early one autumn, with a small backpack and a notebook, I set out to walk an arc across Western Europe that would pass through sunlit coasts, river cities, and hilltop towns where stone churches kept time in bell tolls. My plan was deliberately loose: take trains where possible, linger in cafés, and look for untold corners that a guidebook might mention only in passing. What follows is the path I took and the practical details I noted along the way—landmarks, routes, meals, and tiny local tips you can use to orient yourself or to test a retrieval-based system after embedding this text.

Lisbon — Start on the Seven Hills
I arrived in Lisbon in late September, when the air still held summer’s warmth but mornings were cool enough for a light jacket. The city’s rhythm is set by trams clacking up steep streets and the Tagus River’s wide sweep. Stay in Alfama if you want narrow lanes, fado echoing from tavern doors, and sunrise light on terracotta roofs; choose Chiado or Baixa for easier metro access and more cafés.

Must-see:

São Jorge Castle (Castelo de São Jorge): a fortress with sweeping city views; best to go early to avoid queues.
Belém Tower and Jerónimos Monastery: both in the Belém neighborhood, reachable by tram 15 or a riverside walk of about 30–40 minutes from Praça do Comércio.
Food:
Pastéis de nata are a local specialty—cream tarts best eaten warm. Try them with a bica (espresso).
Practical:
Lisbon’s metro and trams use the Viva Viagem card for contactless travel; a 24-hour public transport pass is useful if you plan several trips in a day.
Porto — Douro and Port
A three-hour train ride north brings you to Porto, a city of steep streets and the Douro River’s deep blue ribbon. If you arrive by train, Porto São Bento station is an attraction itself: tiled murals (azulejos) depict historical scenes.

Must-see:

Ribeira riverside: a UNESCO-listed neighborhood with colorful houses.
Port wine cellars across the river in Vila Nova de Gaia; many offer tastings and brief guided tours.
Food:
Francesinha: a hearty sandwich with melted cheese and a beer-based sauce—available in many traditional cafés.
Practical:
Walk across Luís I Bridge at sunset for spectacular views; the upper deck is pedestrian-friendly.
Seville — Southern Warmth and Alcázar Gardens
From Porto, I took a longer journey south. Seville’s orange trees perfume the streets; patios and tiled courtyards give the city a Mediterranean glow. Spring and early autumn are ideal—summers can be very hot.

Must-see:

Real Alcázar: a palatial complex with intricate Mudéjar architecture and gardens; pre-book tickets online.
Plaza de España: a semi-circular building with tiled alcoves representing Spanish provinces—great for photos.
Food:
Tapas culture here is lively—try pescaíto frito (fried small fish) and jamón ibérico.
Practical:
The historic center is largely walkable, but taxis and trams are convenient for longer hops. Siesta still influences opening hours for smaller shops.
Barcelona — Modernism and Beaches
Heading east along the Mediterranean coast brings you to Barcelona. The city blends modernist architecture, broad boulevards, and a coastline. Timing your visit in shoulder season offers fewer crowds at major sites.

Must-see:

Sagrada Família: Gaudí’s unfinished masterpiece; book a time slot in advance.
Park Güell: offers panoramic city views and Gaudí mosaics; access is ticketed.
Food:
Try a morning “cafè con leche” and share plates of patatas bravas and anchovies for tapas.
Practical:
The T-10 ticket (multi-person travel card) is handy for several rides, and Barcelona’s metro is efficient for covering longer distances.
Nice & Provence — Riviera and Lavender Hills
A short hop by train from Barcelona (or a quick flight) is Nice, where promenades hug the coastline and the old town’s markets burst with fruit and Provençal scent. Rent a car or use regional trains to reach small Provence villages.

Must-see:

Promenade des Anglais in Nice for seaside walking.
Saint-Paul-de-Vence and Èze: perched hilltop villages with art galleries and cobbled streets; public buses connect them to Nice and Monaco.
Food:
Salade niçoise and socca (chickpea pancake) are local staples.
Practical:
Trains in Provence can be infrequent on Sundays—check schedules in advance.
Florence — Renaissance Streets
In central Italy, Florence is a concentrated museum of Renaissance art. The city center is compact; you can move between major sites on foot.

Must-see:

Uffizi Gallery and Accademia (Michelangelo’s David): both require advance reservations during peak season.
Florence Cathedral (Duomo): climb the dome for close-up views of the city and Brunelleschi’s engineering.
Food:
Try ribollita (Tuscan vegetable soup) and bistecca alla fiorentina (Florentine steak) at traditional trattorias.
Practical:
Trains to nearby Siena and Pisa are straightforward day trips; buy tickets early for faster regional trains.
Rome — Layers of History
Rome is an open-air timeline—ancient ruins in the shadow of baroque churches and modern cafés. Expect crowds around the Colosseum and Vatican areas.

Must-see:

Colosseum and Roman Forum: combined tickets manage access to multiple ancient sites.
Vatican Museums and St. Peter’s Basilica: book the Vatican Museum slot online; climb to St. Peter’s dome for a view of the city.
Food:
Carbonara and cacio e pepe are Roman classics—seeking a small, family-run osteria often yields the best versions.
Practical:
Walk whenever possible; many streets near tourist hubs are narrow. Beware of pickpockets in crowded areas and on public transport.
Cinque Terre — Cliffside Villages
From Florence or Rome, head to the Ligurian coast and the Cinque Terre for cliffside hiking and pastel houses tumbling to the sea. The five villages—Riomaggiore, Manarola, Corniglia, Vernazza, and Monterosso—are connected by coastal trails and a local train.

Must-see:

Hiking trail between Vernazza and Monterosso offers coastal panoramas; sections may close seasonally—check local notices.
Food:
Pesto alla Genovese originates nearby; try trofie al pesto in a village trattoria.
Practical:
A Cinque Terre Card grants access to trails and some transport; book accommodation in Monterosso for the widest choice of hotels.
Zurich — Swiss Precision and River Walks
Switzerland feels different—clean, quiet, and on time. Zurich’s lake, river, and tram network make it an efficient city to explore.

Must-see:

Take a boat on Lake Zurich for a calm perspective of the city and surrounding hills.
Old Town (Altstadt) for medieval lanes and niche museums.
Food:
Try rösti (potato dish) and observe coffee pricing—expect higher costs compared to southern Europe.
Practical:
Zürich Card provides free public transport and discounted museum entry; trains to Lucerne or the Alps are punctual and frequent.
Amsterdam — Canals and Cycling
Amsterdam’s canals and bicycle culture define the city. Narrow houses line waterways and museums host vast art collections.

Must-see:

Rijksmuseum and Van Gogh Museum—both have timed tickets.
Anne Frank House: book far in advance.
Food:
Herring stands and stroopwafels are street-food curiosities; a mid-range café meal is reasonably priced.
Practical:
Rent a bike to explore like a local, but stay in bike lanes and follow traffic norms; trams are frequent for longer trips.
Prague — Gothic and Golden Lanes
In Central Europe, Prague’s cobblestone streets and spired skyline feel like a fairy tale. The Old Town Square and Charles Bridge are central hubs.

Must-see:

Prague Castle complex and the Astronomical Clock in Old Town Square; arrive early to avoid the biggest crowds.
Food:
Goulash and dumplings provide hearty fuel for walking cobbled hills; beer is a cultural staple and affordably priced.
Practical:
The city has an efficient metro and tram network; a short-distance ticket is valid on all trams and buses.
Practical Tips That Traveled With Me
Seasons and packing:

Spring (April–June) and autumn (September–October) are the sweet spots for comfortable weather and thinner crowds.
Pack layers: even in late summer, evenings in northern coastal cities can be cool.
Trains and passes:
Book high-speed trains (e.g., TGV, AVE, Frecciarossa) in advance for best prices; regional trains can be purchased on the day.
Consider a rail pass only if you’ll take many long, last-minute journeys—point-to-point tickets are often cheaper.
Money and phones:
Many places accept contactless cards, but small cafés and markets may prefer cash. A local SIM or an eSIM with data is useful for maps and tickets.
Museums:
Reserve timed-entry tickets online for big museums; arrive 15 minutes early.
Local etiquette:
In southern Europe, dinner often starts later (8pm+); in northern Europe, shops may close earlier on Sundays.
Safety:
Europe is generally safe, but common-sense precautions against pickpocketing in tourist-dense areas are wise.
